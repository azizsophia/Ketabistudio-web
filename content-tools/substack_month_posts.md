## POST 1 — Tuesday, week 1
**SEO title:** What Does Rahma Mean? Why Mercy and the Womb Share One Arabic Root
**Subtitle:** The word behind Bismillah, and what it quietly tells you about God
**Target keyword:** meaning of rahma / ar-Rahman ar-Raheem meaning
**Meta description:** Rahma is usually translated as mercy. Its root also gives the word for womb. Here is what that connection means, with sources.
**Tags:** rahma, ar-Rahman, arabic root words, quran, islam

You say it more than any other line. Before you eat, before you drive, before you begin
almost anything: Bismillah ar-Rahman ar-Raheem. In the name of Allah, the Most Merciful,
the Most Compassionate.

Both of those names, ar-Rahman and ar-Raheem, come from one three letter root: ra, ha,
meem. ر ح م. And here is the thing most people never notice. That same root gives another
everyday Arabic word: rahim, the womb.

This is not a coincidence of spelling. The Prophet, peace be upon him, said that the womb
(rahim) is derived from the name of the Most Merciful, ar-Rahman, and that Allah said of
it, "Whoever keeps you, I will keep him, and whoever cuts you off, I will cut him off"
(Sahih al-Bukhari 5988; also Jami' at-Tirmidhi 1907, graded sahih).

Sit with what that means. The first mercy you ever knew had a name built from His mercy.
Before you had a face, a name, or a single deed to your record, you were held inside a
thing named after the most merciful attribute of God. Your beginning was already an act of
rahma.

And His mercy did not stop at your beginning. He says, "My mercy encompasses all things"
(Qur'an 7:156). Not most things. All things. The word choice is deliberate. Rahma in Arabic
is not a thin, polite kindness. It is the tenderness that acts, the softness that protects
and provides.

This is why studying Arabic roots changes how prayer feels. You are not learning vocabulary.
You are learning that the words your prayers are already made of were chosen with a care you
can trace, letter by letter, back to their source.

That is the whole idea behind From One Root, our 30 day journal. One Arabic root a day, traced
back to its classical meaning, with room to write what it stirs in you. Rahma is day one. If
this is the kind of thing that makes you slow down, you can find it on our Etsy.

Tomorrow you will say Bismillah again. Now you know a little more about the mercy you are
naming.

## POST 2 — Friday, week 1
**SEO title:** 6 Authentic Duas for Anxiety and a Heavy Heart (With Sources)
**Subtitle:** Short, memorisable, and every one traced to Qur'an or Sahih hadith
**Target keyword:** dua for anxiety / dua for stress and worry
**Meta description:** Six authentic duas for anxiety, grief, and a heart that will not settle. Each with its Arabic, meaning, and graded source.
**Tags:** dua for anxiety, islamic duas, mental health islam, quran, hadith

When the chest tightens, you do not want a lecture. You want words you can actually say. Here
are six, all short enough to hold onto, and every one traced to a strong source. No made up
duas, no weak chains.

**1. For anxiety and grief directly.** The Prophet, peace be upon him, taught: "Allahumma inni
a'udhu bika minal-hammi wal-hazan." O Allah, I seek refuge in You from anxiety and grief
(Sahih al-Bukhari 6369). He named the two feelings exactly: hamm, the worry about what is
coming, and hazan, the grief over what has passed.

**2. When something is taken from you.** "Inna lillahi wa inna ilayhi raji'un, Allahumma'jurni
fi musibati wa akhlif li khayran minha." To Allah we belong and to Him we return. O Allah,
reward me in my affliction and replace it for me with something better (Sahih Muslim 918).

**3. For a frightened heart at night.** "A'udhu bikalimatil-lahit-tammati min sharri ma khalaq."
I seek refuge in the perfect words of Allah from the evil of what He has created
(Sahih Muslim 2708).

**4. When your faith itself feels shaky.** "Ya muqallibal-qulubi thabbit qalbi 'ala dinik." O
Turner of hearts, make my heart firm upon Your religion (Jami' at-Tirmidhi 3522, hasan). The
Prophet said this often, which is its own quiet comfort. Even hearts near the source needed
steadying.

**5. When everything feels too hard.** "Allahumma la sahla illa ma ja'altahu sahla, wa anta
taj'alul-hazna idha shi'ta sahla." O Allah, there is no ease except what You make easy, and
You make the difficult, if You will, easy (Sahih Ibn Hibban 2427, graded sahih).

**6. When you have to let go and trust.** "Hasbunallahu wa ni'mal-wakil." Allah is sufficient
for us, and He is the best Disposer of affairs (Qur'an 3:173).

Save this list. Better, put one where you will see it. We make a set of these as phone
wallpapers and a printable du'a deck, both drawn from authentic sources, if you would like
them somewhere you will actually look. The Ketabi app keeps them in your pocket too, free.

He is nearer than the worry. "Truly, in the remembrance of Allah do hearts find rest"
(Qur'an 13:28).

## POST 3 — Tuesday, week 2
**SEO title:** What Does Fitra Mean in Islam? The Faith You Were Born Knowing
**Subtitle:** Not something you build. Something you return to.
**Target keyword:** fitra meaning / what is fitra in islam
**Meta description:** Fitra is your original nature, the faith you were created upon. Here is what the word means and where it comes from, with sources.
**Tags:** fitra, islam, quran 30:30, arabic meaning, iman

There is a word for the feeling you get on a clear night, or holding a newborn, when
something in you says, without being taught, that all of this is from Someone. That word is
fitra.

Allah says, "So direct your face toward the religion, inclining to truth: the fitra of Allah
upon which He created all people" (Qur'an 30:30). Your fitra is your original nature. The
faith you were made with, before the noise.

The Prophet, peace be upon him, said, "Every child is born upon the fitra" (Sahih al-Bukhari
1358). Belief is not the thing you have to install from the outside. It is the thing you were
already carrying, that life slowly covered over.

The root itself carries this. Fitra comes from fa, ta, ra: to split open, the way dawn splits
the night, and to bring something into being for the first time. Your fitra is your first
nature. Which means faith, for most of us, is less about construction and more about
excavation. Less building, more remembering.

That reframes the hard days. When belief feels far, you are not failing to manufacture
something new. You are being asked to clear away what settled on top of something that was
always there. That is a gentler task, and an honest one.

This is why we built our journaling around Arabic roots instead of around rules. Fitra is one
of the thirty roots in From One Root, our 30 day journal. You trace the word to its source,
then write about where you feel it covered over in your own life, and what might clear it. It
is on our Etsy if that speaks to you.

You were born knowing. The work is remembering.

## POST 4 — Friday, week 2
**SEO title:** How to Start Quran Journaling: A Gentle 30-Day Method
**Subtitle:** For anyone who opens the Qur'an, means to reflect, and then just reads and closes it
**Target keyword:** quran journaling / how to journal the quran
**Meta description:** A simple, sustainable way to start Quran journaling, built around one Arabic word a day. No pressure, no perfection.
**Tags:** quran journaling, islamic journaling, quran reflection, muslim habits

Most Qur'an journals fail for the same reason most journals fail. They ask for too much on day
one. A blank page and the instruction "reflect" is a fast way to write nothing.

Here is a method that lasts, because it gives you a small, fixed starting point every day.

**Start with one word, not one page.** Instead of a whole surah, take a single meaningful
Arabic word. Rahma. Sabr. Shukr. Small enough to actually think about.

**Trace it to its root.** Almost every Qur'anic word grows from a three letter root that
carries a picture. Shukr, gratitude, shares its root with the idea of a plant that grows in
poor soil. Learn the root and the word stops being abstract.

**Write to one question, not a blank page.** A prompt does the heavy lifting. For shukr:
"Name one provision you have stopped noticing." You will always have an answer to a real
question.

**Anchor it in one verse.** End with a single ayah that holds the word. For gratitude:
"If you are grateful, I will surely increase you" (Qur'an 14:7). Now the word has a home in
the text.

**Keep it to ten minutes.** The goal is not a beautiful entry. It is tomorrow's entry. Small
and daily beats big and rare, every time.

Do that for thirty days and something shifts. You stop reading over words like rahma and taqwa
and start recognising them, the way you recognise a friend across a room.

This is exactly how we built From One Root, our 30 day Qur'an journal. Each day gives you one
root, its classical meaning with the source cited, a verse, and a prompt, with room to write.
It takes the method above and does the setup for you, so all you bring is ten honest minutes.
It is on our Etsy.

You do not need to be a scholar to reflect on the Qur'an. You need a small door in, every day.

## POST 5 — Tuesday, week 3
**SEO title:** Teaching Children the Names of Allah: Where to Actually Begin
**Subtitle:** Start with the two your child already needs, not all ninety nine at once
**Target keyword:** teaching kids the names of Allah / 99 names of Allah for kids
**Meta description:** A calm, doable way to teach children about Allah, starting with the names they can feel before they can memorise.
**Tags:** names of allah for kids, islamic parenting, muslim kids, tarbiyah

The instinct is to hand a child a poster of all ninety nine names and hope it sticks. It rarely
does, because a name your child cannot feel is just a word to recite.

Allah says, "And to Allah belong the best names, so call upon Him by them" (Qur'an 7:180). The
door He opens is calling upon Him, relationship, not memorisation. So start there.

**Begin with ar-Rahman and ar-Raheem.** The Most Merciful, the Most Compassionate. Your child
says these every time they say Bismillah. Connect the word to something they know: the mercy
they feel from you is a small echo of His. He is the source your love points back to.

**Then al-Wadud, the Loving.** Not just that Allah is merciful when you obey, but that He is
loving, warmly and by nature. For a child, knowing they are loved by God before they have
earned anything is the safest floor to stand on.

**Then al-Khaliq, the Creator.** Children already ask who made the moon, the ant, their own
hands. "It is He who created for you all that is on the earth" (Qur'an 2:29). You are not
introducing a hard idea. You are naming a question they already have.

Notice the order. You lead with the names that make a child feel held, then the ones that make
them wonder. Awe grows best out of safety.

This is the heart of our storybooks and the Ketabi app: introducing Allah to children through
what they can feel first, gently and without fear. If you want help starting these
conversations, the app is free, and our personalised storybooks make your own child the one
being spoken to.

You are not trying to make your child memorise God. You are trying to help them love Him. Start
with the names that make that easy.

## POST 7 — Tuesday, week 4
**SEO title:** Islamic Wishes and Duas for a New Baby (What to Say and Write)
**Subtitle:** The words to say to new parents, and what to write in the card
**Target keyword:** islamic new baby wishes / dua for newborn baby
**Meta description:** What to say and write for a new baby in Islam, including an authentic dua for protection and beautiful congratulations.
**Tags:** new baby islam, dua for newborn, islamic congratulations, muslim baby

A new baby leaves most of us reaching for words. "Congratulations" feels too small for
something this big. Here is what our tradition gives you to say and to write.

**A dua of protection over the child.** The Prophet, peace be upon him, would seek refuge for
children with these words: "U'idhuka bikalimatil-lahit-tammati min kulli shaytanin wa hammah,
wa min kulli 'aynin lammah." I seek refuge for you in the perfect words of Allah from every
devil and poisonous creature, and from every harmful, envious eye (Sahih al-Bukhari 3371). A
beautiful thing to say over a newborn, or to write for the parents to keep.

**A congratulation with weight.** A known supplication taught for new parents is: "Baraka
Allahu laka fil-mawhubi lak, wa shakartal-wahib, wa balagha ashuddah, wa ruziqta birrah." May
Allah bless you in the gift given to you, may you give thanks to the Giver, may the child reach
maturity, and may you be granted its righteousness. It asks for the whole arc of the child's
life, not just the happy day.

**Name the mercy.** Even a simple line lands when it is true: "May Allah make this child a
coolness of your eyes and a means of Jannah for you both." It echoes the du'a of the
believers in the Qur'an, "Our Lord, grant us from among our spouses and offspring comfort to
our eyes" (Qur'an 25:74).

You do not need to be poetic. You need to be sincere and specific. Say the child's name. Ask
for something real.

## POST 8 — Friday, week 4
**SEO title:** What to Write in an Islamic (Nikah) Wedding Card
**Subtitle:** The Prophet's own wedding dua, and how to say it in your own words
**Target keyword:** nikah card wishes / islamic wedding dua
**Meta description:** What to write in a nikah or Islamic wedding card, including the authentic prophetic dua for newlyweds and warm ways to phrase it.
**Tags:** nikah wishes, islamic wedding dua, muslim wedding, barakah

"Congratulations on your wedding" is fine. But for a nikah, our tradition hands you something
far better to write, and it takes one line.

**The prophetic dua for newlyweds.** When someone married, the Prophet, peace be upon him,
would say: "Barakallahu laka, wa baraka 'alaika, wa jama'a baynakuma fi khayr." May Allah bless
you, and send blessings upon you, and join you both together in goodness (Jami' at-Tirmidhi
1091, graded sahih; also Sunan Abi Dawud 2130).

Look closely at what it asks for. Not just happiness. Barakah, blessing, the quiet increase
that makes a little go far and a hard year hold. And notice it asks for blessing both in the
good times (baraka laka) and upon them in the hard ones (baraka 'alaika). It is a dua built for
a whole marriage, not a wedding day.

**How to write it in a card.** You can write the Arabic, then the meaning, then one honest line
of your own. Something like: "May Allah bless you both, and join you together in goodness. May
your home always have room for one more guest and one more dua."

**Keep your own line specific.** Skip the generic. If you know the couple, say the true thing:
that you have watched them become gentler, that you prayed for this, that their home already
feels like a place people are safe.

That is the difference between a card that gets recycled and one that ends up in a drawer,
kept. Weight comes from a real dua and a real sentence.
